A perfect number theory proof

October 8, 2021
  • maths
  • proof

For my first article on this blog (or any blog for that matter), I want to present one of my favorite proofs of elementary number theory. It’s based around the concept of a perfect number. You know that a concept named like that ought to be interesting. A perfect number is any positive integer that is equal to the sum of its positive divisors, except the fact that you don’t count the number itself in that sum. You only sum its proper divisors. The whole is equal to the sum of its parts; that’s why such a number is perfect. For instance, six is a perfect number because its proper divisors are 1, 2 and 3; if you sum these up you get back six. In fact six is the smallest perfect number. The next one is 28 as its proper divisors are 1, 2, 4, 7 and 14.

Six is a number perfect in itself, and not because God created all things in six days; rather, the converse is true. God created all things in six days because the number is perfect.

– The City of God by Saint Augustine

The Euclid-Euler theorem

If we introduce the notation σ(n)\sigma(n) to mean “the sum of all the positive divisors of nn, including nn itself”, then a number nn is perfect if and only if σ(n)=2n\sigma(n)=2n. For instance, the obvious fact that a prime number cannot be perfect can be translated as saying that for all primes pp, we have σ(p)=p+1\sigma(p)=p+1, which is never equal to 2p2p.

An interesting fact about σ\sigma is that it’s a multiplicative function. It means that if nn and mm are two coprime numbers (they have no prime divisors in common), then σ(nm)=σ(n)σ(m)\sigma(nm)=\sigma(n)\sigma(m). Intuitively, that happens because the set of divisors of nn is essentially disjoint from the set of divisors of mm — a consequence of coprimality. Therefore each divisor of nmnm can be expressed as a product d1d2d_1d_2, where d1d_1 divides nn and d2d_2 divides mm. Since you can express a sum of products as a product of sums, well, you get the idea.

Euclid’s part

In his Elements, three hundred years before Jesus Christ was born, Euclid proved that if 2p−12^p-1 is prime, then any number nn of the form 2p−1(2p−1)2^{p-1}(2^p-1) must be perfect. Using our characterization of perfect numbers using σ\sigma and the fact that it’s a multiplicative function, that’s easy to prove:

σ(n)=σ(2p−1)σ(2p−1)=(∑k=1p−12k)⋅2p=(2p−1)⋅2p=2n.\begin{align}\sigma(n)&=\sigma(2^{p-1})\sigma(2^p-1)\\&=(\sum_{k=1}^{p-1}2^k)\cdot2^p\\&=(2^p-1)\cdot2^p\\&=2n.\end{align}

A prime number of the form 2p−12^p-1 is called a Mersenne prime. Euclid’s result means that each Mersenne prime gives rise to a perfect number. It is not known whether or not there are infinitely many Mersenne primes, but if there were, it would prove that there are infinitely many perfect numbers.

Euler’s part

About two millenia after Euclid, Euler proved that any even perfect number must be of the form 2p−1(2p−1)2^{p-1}(2^p-1) with 2p−12^p-1 prime. To prove this, he used a simple proof with a very clever idea at a key moment. I still remember the first time I saw this proof. I still find it fascinating. We start with nn, an even perfect number. First, we compute σ(n)\sigma(n) in two different but equal ways. The first way is trivial: because nn is perfect, we know that σ(n)=2n\sigma(n)=2n. The second way uses the fact that nn is even: we write nn as 2k−1m2^{k-1}m, with k>1k>1 and mm odd. Now

σ(n)=σ(2k−1)σ(m)=(2k−1)σ(m).\begin{align}\sigma(n)&=\sigma(2^{k-1})\sigma(m)\\&=(2^k-1)\sigma(m).\end{align}

The two ways of looking at σ(n)\sigma(n) are equal, therefore

2km=(2k−1)σ(m).2^km = (2^k-1)\sigma(m).

We get two things from this equation. Firstly, since 2k−12^k-1 is coprime to 2k2^k, we know that 2k−12^k-1 must divide mm. We write m=(2k−1)Mm=(2^k-1)M for some integer MM. Secondly, we get that σ(m)=2kM\sigma(m)=2^kM just by dividing both sides by 2k−12^k-1 and substituting (2k−1)M(2^k-1)M for mm. Putting these two things together, we have σ(m)=m+M\sigma(m)=m+M. Here is the clever part! This means that mm has only two divisors. The only integers with exactly two divisors are the primes, and so mm is a prime and M=1M=1. By substituting 11 for MM in the above equation, we finally obtain m=2k−1m=2^k-1. Note that MM is different from mm since m=(2k−1)M>(21−1)Mm = (2^k-1)M > (2^1-1)M. That’s in fact why we need nn to be even, because it guarantees that k>1k>1.

Putting it together

By taking Euclid’s and Euler’s parts and putting them together, we get a characterization of the even perfect numbers! An even number nn is perfect if and only if there exists a prime number 2p−12^p-1 such that

n=2p−1(2p−1).n = 2^{p-1}(2^p-1).

This is called the Euclid-Euler theorem. Interestingly, it is unknown if there are any odd perfect numbers… but if there are, the smallest one must be greater than 1015010^{150}.

As I mentioned briefly above, that means that you can use Mersenne primes to generate perfect numbers. From GIMPS, the Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search, we know for instance that

277232917−12^{77232917}-1

is a prime. Therefore,

277232916(277232917−1)2^{77232916}(2^{77232917-1})

is perfect. It ends with 6.
